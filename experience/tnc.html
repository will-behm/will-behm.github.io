My Work at The Nature Conservancy

The summer after graduating from Middlebury, I completed an internship with The Nature Conservancy (TNC)'s Tri-State Chapter (MN, ND, SD). My title was Resilient Waters Intern, and I worked across projects for the Chapter's Resilient Waters team.

Key Responsibilities

  • Prairie Plant Surveys
  • Peatland Carbon Flux Monitoring
  • Wild Rice (Manoomin) Monitoring
  • GIS Data Management
  • Prairie Stream Assessment and Strategy Prioritization

Challenges & What I Learned

Throughout my time at TNC, I encountered unique challenges, such as:

  • - Creating Cartograpic products for project stakeholder meetings.
  • - Learning and implementing complex field protocols for stream surveys, plant surveys, and peatland research.
  • - Data analysis for Plant community surveys in R.
  • - working with a host of diffent voices, stakeholders, and interest groups on projects, including multiple indigenous tribes leading conservation projects.

Fieldwork

TNC's peatland research required that we go out into the Sax-Zim Bog of Nothern MN, in order to measure peat depth, water level, plant diversity, and carbon flux. This was my favorite fieldwork of the summer, despite the thick swarms of mosquitoes and unique scent of methane that can come off of the bog.

Measuring peat depth and water table height with TNC's Restoration Ecologist and Freshwater Fellow.

A restoration project to speed peat recovery in a ditch dug with the intention of draining the surrounding bog. This was common practice for white settlers attempting to grow crops. It not only damaged the connectivity of the bog ecosystem, but also tended to fail miserably.

We also monitored wild rice extent and health as part of a project led by Minnesota's Department of Natural Resources and a coalition of indigenous tribes. The rice primarily grows in MN's uniquely shallow lakes, many of which have been cleared for recreation purposes. Not only is the Wild Rice totally delicious, its of massive cultural significance to the tribes and a critical component of MN's lake ecosystems. I feel super lucky that I got the chance to learn about it from folks who have harvested it since time immemorial.

Heading out for a day of rice monitoring - all of our work was done from a canoe, because its the best way to work in the rice without damaging it. It's a surprisingly good core workout to spend hours paddling and balancing yourself to complete protocols.

One of the bigger field projects we undertook was asessing a few miles of Mud Creek, a praire stream in central MN, and developing a conservation strategy and prioritization report including our findings and all other available asessments of the reach. We assessed channel and bank erosion, and made detailed notes on biota, vegetation, and riparian conditions.

Walking a stretch of Mud Creek with TNC's Freshwater Fellow during a survey. The haze above us was actually from one of TNC's prescribed burns happening on a praire preserve nearby!
